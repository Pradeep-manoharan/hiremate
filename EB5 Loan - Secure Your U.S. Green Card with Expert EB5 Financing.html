Your Career Shouldn't Depend on One Employer

The H-1B system ties your professional future to your sponsor. This dependency limits flexibility, amplifies risks, and leaves many skilled professionals vulnerable to uncertainty.

Lack of Growth Opportunities

As an H1B visa holder, you are tied to a specific employer. To change jobs, you need another employer to sponsor your visa, limiting your career mobility.

Risk of Unemployment

If you lose your job, you need to find a new employer to sponsor your visa within 60 days. Otherwise, you may need to leave the country, disrupting your life.

Employer Exploitation

The dependency on a single employer can sometimes lead to exploitation. Employers may take advantage of H1B visa holders’ uncertain status.

Green Card Backlogs and Long Waiting Periods

Even with pathways like EB-1A, O-1, or NIW, visa holders often remain in prolonged temporary status due to navigating renewals, uncertainty, and backlogs before achieving true permanence.

Long Waiting Periods

The green card application process can take many years, especially for individuals from countries with high demand for US visas.

Annual Caps and Limits

The immigration system in the USA imposes annual caps and per-country limits on the number of green cards issued, leading to significant backlogs.

Temporary Status

H1B visa holders remain in a temporary status until they obtain their green card. It can limit their ability to plan long-term life events.

Challenges for Dependent Family Members

The dependent H-1B visa holders, unmarried children under age 21, and spouses can get H-4 visas to stay with them. They also have to face various challenges, including:

Work Restrictions

H4 visa holders cannot work without an EAD, causing issues.

Visa Status for Children

Children on H4 visas need visa status, complicating their education.

Personal Impact

Financial aid issues after 21 can create stress for H4 visa families.
